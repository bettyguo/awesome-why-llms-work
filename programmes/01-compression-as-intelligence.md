# Programme 01 — Compression-as-Intelligence

> *"Intelligence is compression."* The strong reading: a model's lossless-compression performance over text is the right scalar summary of how good it is. The weaker reading: compression performance and downstream benchmark performance are tightly coupled along the family of decoder-only transformers trained on natural text.

---

## 1. Hard Core (falsifiable claim)

**A model's average lossless-compression rate over a held-out text corpus is approximately linearly related to its average performance on broad downstream benchmarks, across model families and across multiple orders of magnitude of model scale.**

The "linearly related" is doing real work here. The programme's strong form predicts not just a monotone relationship, but specifically that a *linear regression* of benchmark score against bits-per-byte (or against compression ratio) accounts for most of the across-model variance in benchmark score.

If this is right, then:

- Compression is not just *correlated with* intelligence; it is the right scalar summary of it.
- The "objective" of pretraining — next-token prediction — is doing exactly the work it is *supposed* to do under the algorithmic-information-theory framing.
- Improving compression is improving intelligence; metrics like perplexity are not proxies but the thing itself.

The claim is falsifiable: a model family where compression performance and benchmark performance *decouple* — where you can improve one without improving the other, by a non-trivial margin — would refute it.

---

## 2. Protective Belt

When local refutations appear, the programme can absorb them by adjusting any of:

- **Choice of compression corpus.** Compression measured on Wikipedia is not the same as compression measured on code or on math. A weak correlation on one corpus does not refute the programme if a stronger one is available on another. (This is a real soft spot — see "Falsification Status".)
- **Choice of benchmark suite.** The programme is about *average* benchmark performance. A specific benchmark where compression and score decouple (say, an adversarial math benchmark) does not refute the programme by itself.
- **Choice of scale.** The programme is about the scaling regime, not about a single point. Tiny models that score near chance contribute mostly noise; very large reasoning-tuned models can pull the regression line in non-trivial ways.
- **"Linear" vs. "monotone".** The strong form claims *linear*; weaker forms claim only *monotone*. Defenders can retreat from one to the other when pressed.

These are legitimate moves, but each retreat reduces the programme's epistemic content. We track which form of the claim each cited paper actually supports.

---

## 3. Positive Heuristic

Research generated by this programme tends to:

- Treat **bits-per-byte (bpb)** on a fixed corpus as the canonical model-quality scalar, alongside or replacing perplexity.
- Treat **compression ratio** as a model-comparison axis that is more meaningful than parameter count or FLOPs.
- Use **arithmetic coding** with the model as the predictive distribution to actually *operationalize* an LLM as a lossless compressor on non-text modalities (Delétang et al. 2023 famously do this on text, audio, and images).
- Look for **scaling laws of compression** specifically (separate from scaling laws of loss).
- Connect to **Solomonoff induction / AIXI** at the philosophical level — Hutter's framing is the philosophical anchor of the programme.

## 4. Key Papers (annotated)

### Primary sources

- **Language Modeling Is Compression** (2023) — *Delétang, Ruoss, Duquenne, Catt, Genewein, Mattern, Grau-Moya, Wenliang, Aitchison, Orseau, Hutter, Veness*. [arXiv:2309.10668](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.10668).
  - **Programme**: 01
  - **Claim**: Large language models can be used directly as lossless compressors via arithmetic coding, achieving compression rates competitive with or better than gzip/PNG/FLAC on text, images, and audio respectively — making the LLM-as-compressor identity literal, not metaphorical.
  - **Status**: 🟢 Supported
  - **Why it matters**: Promotes the predictor↔compressor identity from a theoretical observation to an empirical procedure you can run. Shifts the burden of proof: anyone arguing intelligence is *not* compression now has to explain why the model literally compresses non-text modalities well.
  - **Falsifies**: none
  - **Reproduces**: [`notebooks/01-compression-ratio-vs-benchmark.ipynb`](../notebooks/01-compression-ratio-vs-benchmark.ipynb)

- **Compression Represents Intelligence Linearly** (2024) — *Huang, Zhang, Shan, He*. [arXiv:2404.09937](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.09937).
  - **Programme**: 01
  - **Claim**: Across 31 open-source LLMs and 12 benchmarks, average benchmark score is approximately a linear function of compression performance (measured by bits-per-byte on external corpora); the linearity holds within and across model families.
  - **Status**: 🟡 Contested (correlation is robust; causal interpretation and out-of-family extrapolation are debated)
  - **Why it matters**: The empirical anchor of the strong-form claim. Anyone arguing compression-is-intelligence cites this paper; anyone arguing against it has to engage with the 31-model cross-family fit. The contestation is about whether the correlation survives when you include reasoning-finetuned and tool-augmented models.
  - **Falsifies**: weaker "compression and benchmark performance are unrelated" claims.
  - **Reproduces**: [`notebooks/01-compression-ratio-vs-benchmark.ipynb`](../notebooks/01-compression-ratio-vs-benchmark.ipynb)

### Philosophical / theoretical anchors

- **Universal Artificial Intelligence: Sequential Decisions Based on Algorithmic Probability** (2005, book) — *Marcus Hutter*. Springer.
  - **Programme**: 01
  - **Claim**: An idealized agent — AIXI — that maximizes expected reward over all computable environments, weighted by Solomonoff prior (= shortest program length = best compression), is the formal optimum for general intelligence. The empirical analogue: better compressors of experience are better general agents.
  - **Status**: ⚪ Open (as a formal theorem about AIXI it is settled; as an empirical prediction about transformers it is open and only loosely operationalized)
  - **Why it matters**: The philosophical anchor that makes the empirical programme *expect* the linear correlation Huang et al. measured. Without Hutter, "compression-is-intelligence" is a slogan; with him, it is a corollary of a formal framework.
  - **Falsifies**: none directly.

- **A Theory of Universal Artificial Intelligence based on Algorithmic Complexity** (2000) — *Marcus Hutter*. [arXiv:cs/0004001](https://arxiv.org/abs/cs/0004001).
  - **Programme**: 01
  - **Claim**: Algorithmic-complexity-theoretic agents are pareto-optimal in a precisely defined sense, motivating compression-as-induction as the unifying principle.
  - **Status**: ⚪ Open (formal; empirical contact remains loose)
  - **Why it matters**: Earlier formal statement of the AIXI framework, useful for the bibliography record.
  - **Falsifies**: none.

### Contested by

- **The Platonic Representation Hypothesis** (2024) — *Huh, Cheung, Wang, Isola*. [arXiv:2405.07987](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.07987).
  - **Programme**: Adjacent (cross-programme tension with 01 and 02)
  - **Claim**: Models trained on different modalities and objectives converge to a shared statistical representation of reality. The convergence is itself the explanation for capability transfer.
  - **Status**: 🟡 Contested
  - **Why it matters**: Offers an *alternative* anchor for cross-modal capability that is not "compression of the joint distribution," but "convergence to an underlying invariant geometry." Compression-as-intelligence and Platonic representation overlap in their predictions but diverge in their failure modes: one predicts capability tracks bits-per-byte even on weird modalities; the other predicts capability tracks geometric alignment.
  - **Falsifies**: a strict reading where bits-per-byte is the *only* axis that matters.

## 6. Open Problems

A concrete starter list. These are PhD-thesis-shaped questions or competent-research-engineer six-month projects.

1. **Reasoning-tuned cohort.** Re-run the Huang et al. 2024 linear-fit on a cohort that includes a recent reasoning-finetuned model series alongside the matched base models. Does the linearity break? In which direction? Is the deviation predictable from the size of the inference-time compute budget?
2. **Domain-specific decoupling.** Construct a benchmark suite where compression of *general web text* should *not* predict performance (e.g., synthetic algorithmic tasks, mathematical theorem-proving). Measure how much the linear fit weakens. The programme survives if the weakening is graceful; it is in trouble if a strong decoupling appears.
3. **Causal manipulation.** Take two checkpoints with identical compression performance but trained on different data mixes. Do they have identical downstream benchmark performance? If they diverge, the relationship is at least partly mediated by data composition rather than by compression alone.
4. **Cross-tokenizer fairness.** Bits-per-byte cancels tokenization choices, but bits-per-token does not. Is the linear relationship in Huang et al. preserved when comparing models with very different tokenizers (e.g., character-level vs. BPE-32k vs. byte-level)?
5. **Multimodal extension.** Delétang et al. compressed text, audio, and images with text-only LMs. Does the same compression-vs-capability linearity hold across modalities for natively multimodal models?
6. **The "almost" in "almost linearly".** Huang et al.'s reported $R^2$ values are high but not unity. What explains the residual? Is the residual structured (e.g., reasoning-flavored benchmarks deviate one way and factual benchmarks deviate the other), and does that structure align with predictions of the other four programmes?
7. **Solomonoff-flavored experiments at scale.** Can we operationalize "shorter programs implementing the same behavior" inside trained models — say, by SAE decomposition giving us a notion of "circuit description length" — and check whether the *programme description length* correlates with capability?

Submit PRs that resolve any of these to the corresponding falsification-event in `tracker/falsification-events.md`.

### Beginner (2 hours)

1. The README of this repo. The five-programme table establishes the comparative context.
2. The first half of *Compression Represents Intelligence Linearly* (Huang et al. 2024) — pages 1–6. Skip the appendix.
3. The introduction of *Language Modeling Is Compression* (Delétang et al. 2023), specifically the arithmetic-coding explanation.
4. The mini-notebook [`notebooks/01-compression-ratio-vs-benchmark.ipynb`](../notebooks/01-compression-ratio-vs-benchmark.ipynb).

### Intermediate (one weekend)

5. The rest of Huang et al. 2024, including the per-benchmark breakdown.
6. The whole of Delétang et al. 2023, including the cross-modality compression experiments.
7. Chapter 1 of MacKay's *Information Theory, Inference, and Learning Algorithms* for the predictor↔compressor identity at the textbook level (Shannon source coding theorem, arithmetic coding).
8. The synthesis essay [`essays/compression-and-superposition.md`](../essays/compression-and-superposition.md).

### Researcher (a month, with the literature)

9. Hutter's *Universal Artificial Intelligence* (2005). Even if you do not buy AIXI as a research target, the chapters on Solomonoff induction are the philosophical context for the empirical programme.
10. Solomonoff's 1964 inductive inference paper, for the *original* motivation.
11. The chain of follow-up critiques on linearity, decoupling experiments, and reasoning-model behavior — tracked in [`tracker/falsification-events.md`](../tracker/falsification-events.md).

## 8. Mini-Experiment

[`notebooks/01-compression-ratio-vs-benchmark.ipynb`](../notebooks/01-compression-ratio-vs-benchmark.ipynb) — *Compression ratio linearly predicts benchmark score on a sample of 5 open models.*

The notebook:

1. Loads 4–5 small open LMs (TinyLlama, Pythia-410m, Pythia-1.4b, GPT-2-medium, Qwen-0.5b).
2. Computes bits-per-byte on a fixed 1MB Wikipedia sample, using each model as the predictor for arithmetic coding.
3. Pulls each model's published score on a fixed benchmark (we use HellaSwag from the HF Open LLM Leaderboard archived snapshot).
4. Plots benchmark vs. bits-per-byte, fits a linear regression, prints $R^2$.

The didactic claim: even on this tiny scale, you can see the qualitative shape Huang et al. measured at 31-model scale.

The honesty paragraph at the bottom of the notebook explains: a 5-point regression is statistical theater for pedagogy; the *real* evidence is in the 31-model fit; what you should leave with is a feeling for what the variables are, not a strong belief in the linearity.
