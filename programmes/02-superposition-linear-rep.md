# Programme 02 — Superposition and the Linear Representation Hypothesis

> *Trained models pack more features than they have dimensions for; those features are recoverable as approximately linear directions in activation space.* The mechanism story behind both compression-as-intelligence and circuits-as-explanation.

---

## 1. Hard Core (falsifiable claim)

Two coupled claims, treated together because most of the empirical work is joint:

**(A) Superposition.** Trained transformers represent more *features* (interpretable, behaviorally relevant variables) than they have neurons in any given layer; specifically, features are encoded as overlapping directions in activation space, exploiting the near-orthogonality of random vectors in high dimensions.

**(B) Linear Representation Hypothesis (LRH).** For a non-trivial subset of features (binary attributes, categorical variables, simple latent concepts), the model represents them as directions whose presence/absence is approximately *linearly* readable from activations.

Together: the model fits many features into too-small a space by overlapping them as approximately linear directions, and those directions are recoverable by linear methods (probes, sparse autoencoders).

The conjunction is falsifiable. Strong refutations would look like:

- A trained model where the number of behaviorally distinct, interpretable features that you can extract is bounded by the layer dimension (no superposition).
- A trained model where every behaviorally relevant attribute requires a non-linear decoder to read out (no LRH).
- A demonstration that the features extracted by sparse autoencoders are *artifacts of the SAE training*, not properties of the underlying model.

---

## 2. Protective Belt

- **"Features" is doing work.** The programme is comfortable defining a feature as whatever a sufficiently good interpretability technique surfaces. Critics call this circular; defenders counter that the *behavioral predictivity* of the features (e.g., causal interventions changing model behavior) anchors the definition.
- **"Approximately linear" can be made arbitrarily weak.** Strong LRH says binary attributes are exactly linear. Weak LRH says some interesting subset of features are linear; the rest are non-linear in well-understood ways (rotations, magnitudes, concept polytopes).
- **Layer matters.** Even strong proponents accept that early layers may behave differently than mid-layers, and that residual-stream representations may be more linear than MLP-internal ones.
- **Probe-finding pathologies.** Linear probes can spuriously succeed when the probe-training data leaks information not actually used by the model (Belinkov et al. critique). Defenders distinguish probing-as-causal-intervention from probing-as-decoding.

The most contested part of the belt is the *universality* sub-claim: that the *same* features show up across model families and scales. Specific universality results (induction heads across model sizes; some monosemantic features across models) are robust; sweeping versions ("all interesting features are universal") are not.

---

## 3. Positive Heuristic

Research generated by this programme:

- **Sparse autoencoders (SAEs)** as the canonical tool for surfacing the directions. Trained to reconstruct activations from a sparse, over-complete dictionary of directions; the dictionary atoms are the candidate "features."
- **Toy models** of superposition — train a tiny network on a known feature distribution with a known sparsity, see what the network does in the bottleneck. This is the controllable analogue of the empirical SAE work on real models.
- **Linear probes** for specific binary or categorical features (truthfulness, sentiment, syntactic category, country-of-origin).
- **Causal validation**: clamp a candidate feature to a fixed value, see whether downstream behavior matches the predicted causal role.
- **Steering vectors / activation patching** — using LRH-validated directions to control model behavior at inference time.

Tools the programme has produced: SAELens, TransformerLens (shared with Programme 03), Neuronpedia, Goodfire Ember, dictionary-learning libraries.

## 4. Key Papers (annotated)

### Foundational

- **Toy Models of Superposition** (2022) — *Elhage, Hume, Olsson, Schiefer, Henighan, Kravec, Hatfield-Dodds, Lasenby, Drain, Chen, Grosse, McCandlish, Kaplan, Amodei, Wattenberg, Olah*. [transformer-circuits.pub](https://transformer-circuits.pub/2022/toy_model/index.html).
  - **Programme**: 02
  - **Claim**: A small network with a bottleneck, trained on synthetic sparse features, can be observed *explicitly* packing more features than it has dimensions; the geometry of the resulting overcomplete dictionary (digons, triangles, pentagons depending on sparsity) is predictable from the feature statistics.
  - **Status**: 🟢 Supported
  - **Why it matters**: The toy-model result is what makes superposition a *mechanistic* claim and not a vague gesture at high-dimensional geometry. You can see the phenomenon happen in something small enough to fully reverse-engineer. Every subsequent SAE-on-real-models paper rests on the conceptual frame established here.
  - **Falsifies**: a strict reading of "neurons are monosemantic" — at the very least, *trained networks under realistic feature sparsity* are routinely polysemantic in this construction.
  - **Reproduces**: [`notebooks/02-toy-superposition.ipynb`](../notebooks/02-toy-superposition.ipynb)

- **The Linear Representation Hypothesis and the Geometry of Large Language Models** (2023) — *Park, Choe, Veitch*. [arXiv:2311.03658](https://arxiv.org/abs/2311.03658).
  - **Programme**: 02
  - **Claim**: A formal statement of LRH separating *embedding-space* directions (where features are sparse linear combinations of unembedding directions) from *concept-space* directions (where features correspond to causal interventions); the two are related by a specific linear map, and the geometry of the unembedding determines the geometry of features.
  - **Status**: 🟢 Supported (for the formal statement; empirical scope still being mapped)
  - **Why it matters**: Until this paper, LRH was a slogan with many incompatible operational meanings. Park, Choe, and Veitch give a careful formalization that distinguishes representational claims from causal claims and makes the geometry testable. Subsequent work either operates inside their frame or has to argue against it.
  - **Falsifies**: hand-wavy versions of LRH that ignored the unembedding↔embedding distinction.

- **Sparse Autoencoders Find Highly Interpretable Features in Language Models** (2023) — *Cunningham, Ewart, Riggs, Huben, Sharkey*. [arXiv:2309.08600](https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.08600).
  - **Programme**: 02
  - **Claim**: Sparse autoencoders trained on intermediate activations of language models recover a dictionary whose individual atoms behave as monosemantic (single-meaning) features under qualitative inspection, supporting both the superposition and LRH claims simultaneously.
  - **Status**: 🟢 Supported (existence; "highly interpretable" is robust across follow-ups)
  - **Why it matters**: The bridge from toy-model superposition to *real* language-model superposition. After this paper, "we can extract interpretable features from large LMs" stopped being a hope and became a baseline. Most of the SAE literature of 2024–2025 builds on the architectural and training recipes here.
  - **Falsifies**: claims that interpretable features cannot be extracted from real LMs by linear methods.
  - **Reproduces**: tangentially by [`notebooks/02-toy-superposition.ipynb`](../notebooks/02-toy-superposition.ipynb).

### Important follow-ups (Anthropic line)

- **Towards Monosemanticity: Decomposing Language Models With Dictionary Learning** (2023) — *Bricken, Templeton, Batson, Chen, Jermyn, Conerly, Turner, Anil, Denison, Askell, Lasenby, Wu, Kravec, Schiefer, Maxwell, Joseph, Hatfield-Dodds, Tamkin, Nguyen, McLean, Burke, Hume, Carter, Henighan, Olah*. [transformer-circuits.pub](https://transformer-circuits.pub/2023/monosemantic-features/index.html).
  - **Programme**: 02
  - **Claim**: At a more careful training regime than Cunningham et al., sparse autoencoders on a one-layer transformer's residual stream produce dictionaries where the *vast majority* of active features are interpretable and behaviorally meaningful; the polysemantic-neuron problem is dissolved at the feature level.
  - **Status**: 🟢 Supported (replicated by the broader Anthropic team and external groups)
  - **Why it matters**: Pushed the field toward "feature-first" interpretability as opposed to "neuron-first" interpretability. After this, "is this neuron polysemantic?" stopped being a useful question; the right unit of analysis became the SAE feature.
  - **Falsifies**: strong neuron-monosemanticity hypotheses.

### Contested by

- **Codebook Features: Sparse and Discrete Interpretability for Neural Networks** (2023) — *Tamkin, Taufeeque, Goodman*. [arXiv:2310.17230](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.17230).
  - **Programme**: 02 (contesting LRH-by-SAE)
  - **Claim**: Sparse-and-discrete codebook features are an alternative interpretability primitive to dense linear directions, suggesting features may be *quantized* rather than continuous-linear in the way LRH presumes.
  - **Status**: 🟡 Contested
  - **Why it matters**: The contestation is conceptual: if discrete codes work as well as or better than continuous linear directions at interpretability, the strong continuous form of LRH is at minimum not necessary. We track the discrete-vs-continuous question separately because it is alive.
  - **Falsifies**: a strict-uniqueness reading of LRH where the linear-decoder operation is the *only* good description.

- **Are Sparse Autoencoders Useful? A Case Study in Sparse Probing** (2025) — *Kantamneni, Engels, Rajamanoharan, Tegmark, Nanda*. [arXiv:2502.16681](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.16681).
  - **Programme**: 02 (contesting practical usefulness of SAE features)
  - **Claim**: On a careful evaluation, SAE features do not consistently outperform simple linear-probe baselines on downstream interpretability tasks; some headline successes do not survive controlled comparison.
  - **Status**: 🟡 Contested
  - **Why it matters**: A serious internal critique from within the MI community. It does not refute superposition or LRH directly — it sharpens what they predict about the *usefulness* of SAE-extracted features. The programme has to answer: are SAEs the right tool, or only one tool among several?
  - **Falsifies**: "SAEs are the obvious right interpretability primitive" — narrower than LRH itself.

> Additional critiques — including probe-pathology critiques and recent papers questioning the universality sub-claim — are being tracked in [`tracker/falsification-events.md`](../tracker/falsification-events.md). Add yours via PR.

| ID | Claim | Status | Best supporting | Best refuting / contesting |
|----|-------|--------|-----------------|----------------------------|
| 02-A | Trained networks pack more features than they have neurons. | 🟢 Supported | Elhage et al. 2022 (toy); Bricken et al. 2023 (real) | none at present |
| 02-B | At least some features are linearly readable from activations. | 🟢 Supported | Park, Choe, Veitch 2023; broad probing literature | trivially false in its strongest form (some features are clearly non-linear) |

## 6. Open Problems

1. **The width-of-SAE problem.** Feature decompositions change with SAE width. Is there a principled choice of width? Is "the right number of features" a model property or an SAE-training property? If it is the latter, what does that say about superposition as a model property?
2. **Causal vs. correlational features.** Most evidence for LRH is correlational (probing). A feature passes the causal bar if intervening on it changes behavior in a predicted way. Build a benchmark where the gap between correlational and causal features is the primary measurement.
3. **Cross-model universality at scale.** Train SAEs on a matched activation site across (say) 6 model families at a matched scale. Do the dictionaries align? By what metric? Without universality, "circuits" lose much of their generality (cross-programme implication with 03).
4. **Non-linear features.** What features are *non-linear* in trained LMs, and how non-linear? Concretely characterize the failures of strong LRH, not just its successes.
5. **Discrete-vs-continuous.** Settle whether codebook features (Tamkin et al.) are a notational variant of dense linear features or a genuine alternative. A clean test: tasks where one beats the other on the same model.
6. **Compositionality.** When the model represents "Paris" and "France" and "capital-of", does it combine them by a known compositional operation (vector addition? a more interesting bilinear form?), and is that operation universal across composition types?
7. **The "200 problems" item.** Some items in Neel Nanda's *200 Concrete Open Problems in Mechanistic Interpretability* are open. Pick one in the SAE / dictionary-learning section.

1. The Anthropic *Toy Models of Superposition* blog post (Elhage et al. 2022). Read the executive summary and the first three sections.

## 8. Mini-Experiment

[`notebooks/02-toy-superposition.ipynb`](../notebooks/02-toy-superposition.ipynb) — *A 2-layer model with a bottleneck packs more sparse features than it has dimensions, recoverable as linear directions.*

The notebook:

1. Defines a sparse-feature synthetic task: $n=20$ features, embedding dimension $d=5$, feature sparsity $p=0.1$.
2. Trains a two-layer model with a $d$-dimensional bottleneck.
3. Visualizes the learned bottleneck columns: at high sparsity you see a digon/triangle/pentagon geometry consistent with Elhage et al.'s analysis.
4. Confirms that a linear probe trained on bottleneck activations recovers individual features at well-above-chance accuracy despite $n > d$.

Reproduces the qualitative Elhage finding. The honesty paragraph at the bottom explains: this is the *cleanest* version of superposition — synthetic features, known sparsity, no language modeling — and the move from this to real LMs is non-trivial.
