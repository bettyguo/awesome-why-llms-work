# Programme 03 — Circuits and the Biology of LLMs

> *Trained transformers contain discoverable, reusable, and largely modular sub-graphs ("circuits") that implement specific behaviors and can be causally validated by ablation.* The "biology" framing — that interpretability is fieldwork inside an organism, not a deduction from architecture — is the methodological heart of the programme.

---

## 1. Hard Core (falsifiable claim)

**For a range of well-specified tasks, the model's behavior on the task is supported by a small, identifiable sub-graph of attention heads, MLP neurons (or SAE features), and edges between them; the sub-graph can be discovered by causal interventions (ablation, activation patching) and is reproducible across runs and, in many cases, across model families.**

The claim has three sub-components:

- **(A) Locality.** A small fraction of components account for most of the model's competence on a specific task.
- **(B) Causal validation.** The sub-graph is not merely correlational; ablating it degrades the behavior in the predicted way, restoring it (with patching) brings it back.
- **(C) Reusability and universality.** The same circuit motifs (induction heads, name-mover heads, copy-suppression heads) appear across tasks and across model families.

Falsifiers:

- A task that resists circuit decomposition: ablation studies that find no small sub-graph carries the behavior; the competence is genuinely distributed across all components.
- A circuit that fails to replicate across an independent training run of the same model.
- A "circuit" that turns out to be an artifact of patching methodology — e.g., the patched activations carry information from outside the claimed circuit.

---

## 2. Protective Belt

- **"A range of well-specified tasks" hedges.** The programme does not claim circuit decomposability for all behaviors. Specific competences (IOI, induction, modular addition, indirect-object handling) are evidenced; *all* behaviors is not the claim.
- **"Small" is calibrated.** Circuits in modern work routinely involve 10s–100s of components; the claim is that this is much less than the total ($\sim 10^6$+ components in a frontier model), not that circuits are tiny.
- **Causal validation methods evolve.** Activation patching, attribution patching, path patching, edge patching — the protective belt accepts that the *method* of causal validation is still improving and that earlier methods can have artifacts the programme later corrects (Conmy et al. is partly a response to this).
- **Universality varies by motif.** Some motifs (induction heads) are robustly universal; others (specific IOI components) are more model-specific. The programme retreats from "all motifs universal" to "some motifs universal" without losing its core.

The most contested part of the belt is the claim that circuit-level descriptions *explain* model behavior, rather than merely *describe* it in a coarsened form. A skeptical reading: a 100-component "circuit" found in a 100-million-parameter model is not all that small, and the description is not predictive of the model's behavior on out-of-distribution inputs.

---

## 3. Positive Heuristic

Research generated by this programme:

- **Circuit discovery** as the primary research operation. Pick a task. Identify components. Validate causally. Write up the circuit.
- **Mechanistic analyses of specific behaviors**: IOI (Wang et al.), modular addition (Nanda et al.), greater-than (Hanna et al.), arithmetic with carry, refusal, sycophancy.
- **Tooling** to automate the discovery: ACDC (Conmy et al.), attribution patching, edge attribution patching, sparse-feature circuits.
- **The "biology of an LLM"** framing — treat the model as an organism whose internal anatomy can be discovered by careful experiments.
- **Cross-model replication** as the universality test.

Tools the programme has produced or relies on: TransformerLens, SAELens, circuit-tracer, Neuronpedia, Goodfire Ember, the Anthropic interpretability tooling (some open, some not).

## 4. Key Papers (annotated)

### Foundational

- **A Mathematical Framework for Transformer Circuits** (2021) — *Elhage, Nanda, Olsson, Henighan, Joseph, Mann, Askell, Bai, Chen, Conerly, DasSarma, Drain, Ganguli, Hatfield-Dodds, Hernandez, Jones, Kernion, Lovitt, Ndousse, Amodei, Brown, Clark, Kaplan, McCandlish, Olah*. [transformer-circuits.pub](https://transformer-circuits.pub/2021/framework/index.html).
  - **Programme**: 03
  - **Claim**: An attention-only transformer's behavior can be decomposed into a "residual stream" plus "circuits" of QK and OV interactions; one-layer attention-only models have analytically tractable circuits; two-layer attention-only models support qualitatively new circuit motifs (notably *induction heads*).
  - **Status**: 🟢 Supported (foundational; the framework is the standard vocabulary of the field)
  - **Why it matters**: Establishes the basic vocabulary — residual stream, QK / OV, write-and-read — that the rest of the programme uses. The two-layer-attention-only setup is small enough to fully reverse-engineer and large enough to host a new motif (induction).
  - **Falsifies**: none; sets the frame.

- **In-context Learning and Induction Heads** (2022) — *Olsson, Elhage, Nanda, Joseph, DasSarma, Henighan, Mann, Askell, Bai, Chen, Conerly, Drain, Ganguli, Hatfield-Dodds, Hernandez, Johnston, Jones, Kernion, Lovitt, Ndousse, Amodei, Brown, Clark, Kaplan, McCandlish, Olah*. [arXiv:2209.11895](https://arxiv.org/abs/2209.11895).
  - **Programme**: 03 (with strong cross-link to 04)
  - **Claim**: A specific two-component circuit — an attention head that detects "this token appeared previously" composed with one that copies the token after the previous occurrence — appears across model scales and accounts for a large fraction of the loss decrease associated with the onset of in-context learning during training.
  - **Status**: 🟢 Supported
  - **Why it matters**: The single most influential circuit-discovery result. Connects circuits to a behavior (ICL) that is itself the subject of an entire other programme (04). The phase-change-during-training signature also seeds the emergence-and-grokking conversation (programme 05).
  - **Falsifies**: claims that ICL is fully distributed or non-mechanistic. Connects to Programme 04 by giving a *mechanistic* candidate that any Bayesian-mixture story has to reconcile with.
  - **Reproduces**: [`notebooks/03-induction-head-discovery.ipynb`](../notebooks/03-induction-head-discovery.ipynb)

- **Interpretability in the Wild: a Circuit for Indirect Object Identification in GPT-2 small** (2022) — *Wang, Variengien, Conmy, Shlegeris, Steinhardt*. [arXiv:2211.00593](https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.00593).
  - **Programme**: 03
  - **Claim**: GPT-2 small implements indirect-object identification (IOI) via a 26-attention-head circuit grouped into 7 functional classes (name movers, S-inhibition heads, induction heads, duplicate token heads, etc.); causal interventions confirm each class plays its predicted role.
  - **Status**: 🟢 Supported
  - **Why it matters**: The paradigm case of an end-to-end circuit walk-through in a real (if small) language model. Establishes the standard methodology: define the task, isolate components by ablation, classify them by behavior, validate causally. Subsequent circuit-discovery papers either follow this template or argue against parts of it.
  - **Falsifies**: claims that a real LM's task behavior cannot be decomposed into a small functional circuit at all.

### Tooling / methods

- **Towards Automated Circuit Discovery for Mechanistic Interpretability** (2023) — *Conmy, Mavor-Parker, Lynch, Heimersheim, Garriga-Alonso*. [arXiv:2304.14997](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.14997).
  - **Programme**: 03
  - **Claim**: A general algorithm (ACDC) for *automating* the kind of manual circuit discovery in Wang et al. 2022, reproducing several known circuits without manual intervention.
  - **Status**: 🟢 Supported (in scope), 🟡 Contested in extension to large models (compute cost; method may miss distributed circuits)
  - **Why it matters**: Shifts circuit work from artisanal to (semi-)automated. The follow-up literature on attribution patching, edge attribution patching, and sparse-feature circuits is downstream of this paper's bet that the discovery operation can be mechanized.
  - **Falsifies**: claims that circuit discovery is inherently a hand-craft.

- **Progress Measures for Grokking via Mechanistic Interpretability** (2023) — *Nanda, Chan, Lieberum, Smith, Steinhardt*. [arXiv:2301.05217](https://arxiv.org/abs/2301.05217).
  - **Programme**: 03 (with strong cross-link to 05)
  - **Claim**: A small transformer learning modular addition can be fully reverse-engineered: the network implements a Discrete-Fourier-Transform based algorithm; "grokking" — the sudden generalization after extended overfitting — is a phase change between two specific internal algorithms, identifiable by progress measures that anticipate the phase change before test loss reflects it.
  - **Status**: 🟢 Supported
  - **Why it matters**: Demonstrates the circuit framework's reach: complete mechanistic explanation of a non-trivial behavior, and that explanation makes *predictions* about generalization dynamics that pure-loss curves cannot make.
  - **Falsifies**: the strongest forms of "we cannot know what neural networks do internally"; also, in conjunction with Power et al. 2022, *Programme 05*'s strong-emergence claim — the grokking transition has a mechanistic cause.

### Contested by

- **Refusal in Language Models Is Mediated by a Single Direction** (2024) — *Arditi, Obeso, Syed, Paleka, Panickssery, Gurnee, Nanda*. [arXiv:2406.11717](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.11717).
  - **Programme**: 03
  - **Claim**: At least one safety-relevant behavior (refusal of harmful requests) is mediated by a *single direction* in the residual stream rather than by a multi-component circuit; ablating the direction removes refusal across many models.
  - **Status**: 🟢 Supported
  - **Why it matters**: This is in tension with the "circuit-as-multi-component-subgraph" framing if you read circuits maximally. The reconciliation: circuits can be one-dimensional sometimes, and the unifying concept is "minimal sufficient causal structure" not "graph cardinality > 1." But this paper sharpens the question of when a *circuit* (graph) is more useful than a *direction* (single dimension).
  - **Falsifies**: the rhetorical reading that "important behaviors are always implemented by complex graphs."

- **Have Faith in Faithfulness: Going Beyond Circuit Overlap When Finding Model Mechanisms** (2024) — *Hanna, Pezzelle, Belinkov*. [arXiv:2403.17806](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.17806).
  - **Programme**: 03
  - **Claim**: Standard "circuit recovery" metrics overestimate circuit faithfulness; many published circuits do not fully capture the underlying behavior under stricter faithfulness measures.
  - **Status**: 🟡 Contested
  - **Why it matters**: An internal critique that sharpens what "the circuit" means: are we recovering a *sufficient* mechanism, a *minimal* one, or something between? The methodological tightening is now standard in follow-up work.
  - **Falsifies**: hand-wavy reads of "the circuit explains the behavior" — without a faithfulness metric, the explanation is underspecified.

> Additional internal critiques on universality, faithfulness, and SAE-vs-circuit discovery are tracked in [`tracker/falsification-events.md`](../tracker/falsification-events.md).

## 6. Open Problems

1. **Circuit OOD prediction.** Build a benchmark where the circuit recovered on distribution must *predict* model behavior on a carefully chosen out-of-distribution variant. Pass rate on this benchmark is the right scaler for "circuit explains behavior."
2. **Cross-family universality at scale.** Replicate any of the canonical circuits (IOI, induction, greater-than) across at least 4 model families at matched scale. Compare graph isomorphism; failing isomorphism, compare functional equivalence.
3. **Distributed vs. local behaviors.** Find a behavior that *resists* circuit decomposition under careful causal validation. (The programme welcomes this — it sharpens what circuit decomposability requires.)
4. **Faithfulness benchmarks.** Define a faithfulness metric that controls for the Hanna et al. 2024 critiques, and re-evaluate the IOI and induction circuit at that metric.
5. **Sparse-feature circuits.** Combine SAE-derived features (programme 02) with circuit-discovery methods (this programme) to produce *feature-level* circuits, where edges are between features and not just between components. Universality at the feature-graph level is a much stronger claim than at the head level.
6. **Anthropic's 2025 biology line.** The Anthropic *Circuit Tracing* and *On the Biology of a Large Language Model* papers (2025) introduce attribution-graph methods at frontier-model scale. Independent replication of any of their case studies (especially the chain-of-thought analyses) is a high-value open project.
7. **Pick from Sharkey et al. 2025.** *Open Problems in Mechanistic Interpretability* (arXiv:2501.16496) catalogs concrete open problems; choose one.

## 8. Mini-Experiment

[`notebooks/03-induction-head-discovery.ipynb`](../notebooks/03-induction-head-discovery.ipynb) — *A small transformer trained on a copy task develops an attention pattern with the induction-head signature.*

The notebook:

1. Loads a small open-source transformer via TransformerLens (e.g., `gpt2-small` or a tiny 2-layer model).
2. Constructs a "random prefix repeats" synthetic dataset: a random sequence concatenated with itself, where the second half is predictable from the first.
3. For each attention head, measures the *induction signature*: high attention from position $i$ to position $j$ where token $t_{j+1} \approx t_i$.
4. Plots the head-by-head induction scores; highlights the head(s) that look like induction heads.
5. Confirms causally by ablating those heads and measuring the loss change on the synthetic task.

Reproduces the qualitative Olsson et al. finding on a budget you can run in 5 minutes. The honesty paragraph at the bottom: a clean induction signature on a synthetic task is the *easy* version of induction-head discovery; finding induction heads on natural text requires more careful interventions and is what Olsson et al. actually did.
