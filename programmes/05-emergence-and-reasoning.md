# Programme 05 — Emergence and Its Discontents (and Reasoning)

> *Some capabilities of large language models appear sharply at specific scales of training compute or model size — not gradually.* The original strong claim (Wei et al. 2022) has been substantially weakened by metric-artifact arguments (Schaeffer et al. 2023); the test-time-compute / reasoning-models wave (o1, R1, s1) has reopened the question along a different axis.

---

## 1. Hard Core (falsifiable claim)

The strong original claim: **for some downstream tasks, model performance is approximately at chance until a critical scale of model parameters or training compute, after which it improves sharply; this discontinuity is not predictable from smaller-scale runs under any continuous metric.**

The strong claim has been substantially weakened. The remaining defensible cores are:

- **(A) Weak emergence (operational).** Under common evaluation metrics (exact-match accuracy, multi-choice success), some capabilities exhibit sharp transitions as a function of scale — useful as a forecasting signal even if the underlying loss is smooth.
- **(B) Metric-artifact claim (Schaeffer et al.).** The apparent discontinuity is a measurement artifact: when measured by a continuous metric (token-edit-distance, log-likelihood of the correct answer), the same capability emerges smoothly.
- **(C) Reasoning-model "emergence."** Allocating *inference-time* compute (chain-of-thought sampling, search, self-consistency) produces capability jumps that are not visible at fixed inference budget — a new axis of "emergence" distinct from the training-scale one.

Strong falsifiers:

- For (A): a demonstration that, under careful evaluation, the canonical "emergent" capabilities are smooth in training compute.
- For (B): a discrete task where *no* reasonable continuous metric exists and the capability is genuinely zero below scale $S^*$ and positive above.
- For (C): test-time compute scaling does not, in fact, produce capability gains on tasks where it has been claimed.

---

## 2. Protective Belt

- **"Some tasks" hedges hard.** The programme has retreated from "many capabilities are emergent" to "certain specific tasks under specific metrics show discontinuities." Each retreat reduces epistemic content.
- **"Sharp" vs. "predictable from extrapolation".** A capability can be sharp and *still predictable* (a known threshold) — but the original Wei et al. claim included unpredictability. Modern usage often drops the unpredictability claim.
- **Continuous-metric arguments.** Schaeffer et al.'s argument is now widely accepted: discontinuities under multi-choice metrics often disappear under continuous ones. Defenders of emergence have to specify *which metric* and *which task* simultaneously.
- **Train-time vs. test-time emergence.** The reasoning-model wave (o1, DeepSeek-R1, s1) reframes "emergence" as *test-time-compute-scaling* phenomena that look discontinuous in capability-per-prompt but are continuous in compute-per-prompt.

The most contested part of the belt is whether the Schaeffer et al. critique *fully* dissolves emergence or only *partially*. The current consensus is partial: metric-artifact accounts for many but not all reported emergent capabilities.

---

## 3. Positive Heuristic

Research generated by this programme:

- **Catalogs of "emergent" capabilities** with scale curves — Wei et al. (original) and follow-ups.
- **Metric-substitution analyses** — replace exact-match with token-edit-distance, log-prob of correct answer, BLEU; observe whether the discontinuity survives.
- **Phase-change analyses** in training — grokking (Power et al.), induction-head formation (Olsson et al.), modular-addition reverse-engineering (Nanda et al.). These are *training-time* phase changes that the emergence literature both produced and now must engage with.
- **Test-time compute scaling laws** — Snell et al. (2024), s1 (Muennighoff et al. 2025), the DeepSeek-R1 line — recast capability as a function of inference-compute budget.
- **Complexity-science framings** — recent work attempts to formalize "emergence" using order parameters and many-body-systems language (Krakauer, Krakauer, Mitchell 2025).

## 4. Key Papers (annotated)

### Foundational — the strong claim

- **Emergent Abilities of Large Language Models** (2022) — *Wei, Tay, Bommasani, Raffel, Zoph, Borgeaud, Yogatama, Bosma, Zhou, Metzler, Chi, Hashimoto, Vinyals, Liang, Dean, Fedus*. [arXiv:2206.07682](https://arxiv.org/abs/2206.07682) (TMLR 2022).
  - **Programme**: 05
  - **Claim**: For a catalog of tasks (modular arithmetic, transliteration, multi-step reasoning), large language models exhibit near-chance performance below a critical scale and substantially-above-chance performance above it; the discontinuity is "unpredictable" in the sense of not being well-fit by smooth extrapolation from small-model runs.
  - **Status**: 🟡 Contested (in its strong unpredictability form) / 🟢 Supported (in its descriptive form: "these curves look sharp at exact-match")
  - **Why it matters**: Defined the phenomenon and the term *emergent ability*. The most-cited single paper of the programme. Schaeffer et al.'s subsequent refutation argues with this paper directly.
  - **Falsifies**: only-smooth-scaling-law views of capability.

### The refutation

- **Are Emergent Abilities of Large Language Models a Mirage?** (2023) — *Schaeffer, Miranda, Koyejo*. [arXiv:2304.15004](https://arxiv.org/abs/2304.15004) (NeurIPS 2023).
  - **Programme**: 05
  - **Claim**: The apparent emergence in Wei et al.'s catalog is largely an artifact of *nonlinear* or *discontinuous* metrics (exact-match accuracy, multi-choice success). Re-evaluating the same model families under continuous metrics (token-edit-distance, log-probability of correct continuation) shows smooth, predictable scaling.
  - **Status**: 🟢 Supported (within scope: the metric-substitution claim is robust)
  - **Why it matters**: The single most important refutation paper in the programme. After Schaeffer et al., the unmodified Wei-et-al-style "emergence" claim is no longer a respectable default in serious work; serious work specifies the metric. Strong-form unpredictable emergence is *refuted*; weaker descriptive forms remain alive.
  - **Falsifies**: claim 05-A (Wei et al.'s strong unpredictability claim) in its full generality.
  - **Reproduces**: [`notebooks/05-emergence-mirage-demo.ipynb`](../notebooks/05-emergence-mirage-demo.ipynb)

### Training-time phase changes (cross-link to programme 03)

- **Grokking: Generalization Beyond Overfitting on Small Algorithmic Datasets** (2022) — *Power, Burda, Edwards, Babuschkin, Misra*. [arXiv:2201.02177](https://arxiv.org/abs/2201.02177).
  - **Programme**: 05 (with cross-link to 03)
  - **Claim**: On small algorithmic datasets, neural networks can fit the training set to near-zero loss while remaining at chance on the test set, and then — much later in training — abruptly transition to perfect test performance ("grokking").
  - **Status**: 🟢 Supported
  - **Why it matters**: The cleanest example of a training-time phase change in deep learning. Until grokking, "the model trains and either generalizes or doesn't" was the default mental model. After grokking, *time-of-generalization* is its own variable to be explained.
  - **Falsifies**: simple "test loss tracks train loss after some smoothing" pictures.

- **Progress Measures for Grokking via Mechanistic Interpretability** (2023) — *Nanda, Chan, Lieberum, Smith, Steinhardt*. [arXiv:2301.05217](https://arxiv.org/abs/2301.05217). (Also cross-listed in programme 03.)
  - **Programme**: 05 (with cross-link to 03)
  - **Claim**: The grokking transition has a *mechanistic* explanation — the network is gradually building a Discrete-Fourier-Transform-based algorithm that abruptly takes over from a memorization mode. Progress measures (internal-state quantities) anticipate the transition before test loss does.
  - **Status**: 🟢 Supported
  - **Why it matters**: Together with grokking itself, this is the field's clearest demonstration that "emergent / sudden" behaviors can have continuous internal causes — the very pattern that Schaeffer et al. argue is at play in the emergence literature.

### Test-time-compute / reasoning models — a different axis of "emergence"

- **Scaling LLM Test-Time Compute Optimally can be More Effective than Scaling Model Parameters** (2024) — *Snell, Lee, Xu, Kumar*. [arXiv:2408.03314](https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.03314).
  - **Programme**: 05
  - **Claim**: For fixed pretraining, allocating *test-time compute* (verifier-based search, sequential revisions) optimally can outperform proportional increases in model size on a range of math and reasoning benchmarks.
  - **Status**: 🟢 Supported (within scope; magnitude of effect contested)
  - **Why it matters**: Reframes the scaling debate. The "emergence" of reasoning behavior in o1-class models is partly a function of how inference-time compute is spent, not just how the model was trained. This opens a new axis for the emergence question.
  - **Falsifies**: claims that capability is determined by training compute alone.

- **DeepSeek-R1: Incentivizing Reasoning Capability in LLMs via Reinforcement Learning** (2025) — *DeepSeek-AI*. [arXiv:2501.12948](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.12948).
  - **Programme**: 05
  - **Claim**: Chain-of-thought reasoning behavior can be *induced* in base models by reinforcement learning on outcome-verified problems (math, coding), without large human-labeled reasoning datasets; the resulting models exhibit capability profiles that look like training-time emergence at the level of evaluation curves but are mechanistically produced by RL on a small number of training tokens relative to pretraining.
  - **Status**: 🟢 Supported (the empirical result)
  - **Why it matters**: Independent replication of the o1-style training recipe in the open. The mechanism — RL with outcome rewards, not process rewards — narrows the space of explanations for "how reasoning emerges." Recasts emergence as something that can be *targeted* by post-training, not waited-for through scaling.

- **s1: Simple Test-Time Scaling** (2025) — *Muennighoff, Yang, Shi, Li, Fei-Fei, Hajishirzi, Zettlemoyer, Liang, Candès, Hashimoto*. [arXiv:2501.19393](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.19393).
  - **Programme**: 05
  - **Claim**: With 1,000 carefully curated reasoning traces and a simple "budget-forcing" decoding rule that pushes the model to continue thinking before answering, a base model attains capability comparable to o1-preview on competition math (MATH, AIME24).
  - **Status**: 🟢 Supported (the empirical result)
  - **Why it matters**: Compresses the recipe to its core: a small reasoning-trace dataset + a decoding-time control. The "emergence" of o1-class behavior turns out to be cheap to elicit once you know the targets, which sharply constrains theories of what makes reasoning emerge.

### Theoretical / complexity-science framings

- **Large Language Models and Emergence: A Complex Systems Perspective** (2025) — *D. Krakauer, J. Krakauer, M. Mitchell*. [arXiv:2506.11135](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.11135).
  - **Programme**: 05
  - **Claim**: Treats "emergence" formally in the sense used in complex-systems science (order parameters, many-body interactions) and argues that current LLM-emergence claims are weak under that strict definition; offers measurement frameworks that would make claims falsifiable.
  - **Status**: 🟡 Contested (the strict-emergence demand is contested; some find it overly demanding)
  - **Why it matters**: A philosophy-of-science intervention from outside the ML field. Useful as a corrective: "emergence" in the strict complex-systems sense is a high bar that most ML uses of the word do not meet. Disciplines the conversation.

- **Learning to Reason with LLMs** (2024) — *OpenAI*. [OpenAI blog](https://openai.com/index/learning-to-reason-with-llms/). (Blog post; no arXiv.)
  - **Programme**: 05
  - **Claim**: Introduces o1: a model family trained to spend additional inference-time compute on chain-of-thought; reports large gains on reasoning benchmarks (AIME, GPQA) attributable to inference-time scaling.
  - **Status**: ⚪ Open (industrial release without full technical report; reproductions by R1, s1 have largely confirmed the headline result)
  - **Why it matters**: The launch of the reasoning-models era. Even without a full paper, the existence of the o1 capability profile reset the empirical landscape that emergence-vs-mirage debates have to engage with.

> Additional results on test-time compute scaling laws, RL-trained reasoners, and overthinking-vs-underthinking trade-offs are being tracked in [`tracker/falsification-events.md`](../tracker/falsification-events.md). Reproductions of the s1 budget-forcing recipe at other model scales are highly welcome.

### Surveys and reviews

- **The Mystery of In-Context Learning** (Zhou et al. 2023; primary in programme 04) covers ICL-flavored emergence specifically.

## 6. Open Problems

1. **Continuous-metric audit at scale.** Re-run Wei et al.'s emergence catalog on modern open model families with continuous metrics. How much of the catalog survives the Schaeffer et al. critique? A modern systematic survey is missing.
2. **Strict-emergence test.** Following Krakauer, Krakauer, Mitchell 2025, define an order parameter for some specific capability (e.g., multi-step symbolic manipulation) and measure its scaling curve. Does any curve clear the strict-emergence bar?
3. **Test-time compute scaling laws across model sizes.** Snell et al. and s1 establish the regime exists. Map the trade-off: for a fixed task, how does the optimal training-compute / test-compute split vary with task difficulty and base-model size?
4. **Reasoning-emergence mechanism.** The s1 result (1k traces are enough) is methodologically suggestive: reasoning is "already there" and the post-training only elicits it. Open: which circuits / features (programme 03 / 02) are activated by the elicitation?
5. **Overthinking and underthinking.** When test-time compute scales, do all tasks benefit, or are there tasks where more thinking hurts? Characterize the failure modes.
6. **Cross-family transfer of post-training reasoning recipes.** s1-style recipes work on Qwen-base. Do they work cross-family? If not, what does that say about whether reasoning is "in" the base model?
7. **Pick from Sharkey et al. 2025.** *Open Problems in Mechanistic Interpretability* (arXiv:2501.16496) lists reasoning-related mechanism questions.

## 8. Mini-Experiment

[`notebooks/05-emergence-mirage-demo.ipynb`](../notebooks/05-emergence-mirage-demo.ipynb) — *The same model family looks "emergent" under exact-match accuracy and "smooth" under token-edit-distance.*

The notebook:

1. Picks a small open model family with multiple scale points (e.g., Pythia at 70M / 160M / 410M / 1.4B).
2. Runs each on a controlled small task — a fixed-form arithmetic prompt or simple transliteration — where exact-match accuracy is the natural "emergent" metric.
3. Records, for each prompt:
   - exact-match accuracy (binary 0/1),
   - token-edit-distance from the gold answer (continuous),
   - log-probability of the gold answer (continuous).
4. Plots all three metrics as a function of scale.
5. Visualizes the discontinuity-vs-smoothness contrast.

Reproduces the qualitative Schaeffer et al. finding on a budget you can run. The honesty paragraph at the bottom: on the *tiny* scales here, neither curve is dramatic, but the *direction* of the effect is the point. Schaeffer et al.'s original argument is at a much larger scale and is what matters for the field; the notebook is a teaching artifact, not the evidence base.
